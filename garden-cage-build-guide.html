Short Verdict

Build the structure like a small farm tunnel with a critter-proof skirt — not like a decorative garden feature. Five to six posts, 24" hardware cloth band, three cattle panel ribs, two simple front doors, no fancy carpentry. This is the version most likely to work, look decent, stay within reason, and not become a cursed side quest.

Assumption: Two 4×4 raised beds already exist side by side, giving a combined footprint of roughly 96" wide × 48" deep. Structure sits flush with the outer edges of the beds.

Mesh strategy: Hardware cloth only where it matters — lower 24" on all sides and lower half of doors. Do not overskin the whole structure unless animals prove they deserve escalation.
✓
Lumber connections — exterior deck screws
Use 2½" to 3" exterior deck screws for all framing connections. Pre-drill near ends to avoid splitting 2x2 stock.
Framing
✓
Hardware cloth — staple first, then fender washers
Staple to position and tension. Then add screws with fender washers every 8–12" for real hold. Staples alone pull out over time.
MeshDon't skip washers
✓
Cattle panel — galvanized wire + conduit clamps
Galvanized wire ties at each post contact point. Conduit clamps over the wire for a solid lock. Heavy zip ties only for mockup — replace before final.
Arch
✓
Door hardware — 2 hinges minimum, one latch per door
Heavy duty exterior hinges. Hook-and-eye latch or simple gate latch on each door. Add a cane bolt to one leaf at the bottom if needed to stop racking.
Doors
✓
Measure actual outer dimensions of both beds
Do not assume 96"×48". Measure before cutting anything. Mark your numbers on the sketch. Beds built DIY rarely hit exact dimensions.
Measure first
✓
Mark the final post footprint
Chalk or stakes. Front three posts: far left, center (door split), far right. Rear posts: at minimum left and right corners. Mark back center if using 6-post layout.
Layout
✓
Cut front posts to 72"
Three front posts at 72". These carry the arch and door frames. If setting into ground or bed sides, account for burial depth in your cut length.
Cut
✓
Set and brace front posts
Attach to bed sides or set alongside. Check plumb on both axes before screwing off. Temporary diagonal bracing helps while you work.
Structure
✓
Set rear posts
Minimum: rear left and rear right at full height (72"). Add rear center if using 6-post layout. Full-height rear corners produce a cleaner, more rigid structure.
StructureFull height recommended
✓
Cut and attach lower side rails at 24"
2x2 or 2x3 horizontal rails at exactly 24" up from the bed top. One on each long side, running the full 48" depth front to back.
Lower Frame
✓
Cut and attach back lower rail at 24"
One 96" back rail, or two shorter pieces meeting at the back center post. Ties rear posts together and gives the hardware cloth a surface to attach to.
Lower Frame
✓
Staple and screw hardware cloth — side zones
Cut ½" hardware cloth to fill the lower 24" on both sides. Staple under tension first. Then screw fender washers every 8–12" along all edges. Bend the bottom edge under the bed frame if possible for a ground seal.
MeshFender washers required
✓
Staple and screw hardware cloth — back zone
Same process on the back lower 24". This section is often skipped and then regretted. Squirrels and rabbits will find it.
Mesh
✓
Check all wire edges — clip and fold sharp ends
Hardware cloth cut edges are sharp. Fold exposed wire ends with pliers or cover with wood strips. Do this before anything else goes near the structure.
Safety
Cattle panel bending: A 16' cattle panel bent over a 4' span will reach about 72" at peak with a natural curve. Two people makes this significantly easier. Wear gloves — the cut ends are brutal.
✓
Dry-fit first cattle panel rib — front position
Bend the panel over the full 48" depth. Check that the peak hits roughly 72". The panel will want to spring — have a helper hold while you check position and mark attachment points.
Arch
✓
Secure front rib to front posts with galvanized wire
Wire at each contact point on both front posts. Conduit clamps over the wire for a solid lock. Check peak alignment before fully tightening.
Arch
✓
Place and secure middle rib
Position at approximately the midpoint of the 48" depth. Tie to top of side rails or to connecting wire running front to back. Keep consistent with front rib peak.
Arch
✓
Place and secure rear rib
Rear rib sits at the back posts. Wire and clamp same as front. Three ribs creates a rigid tunnel — two is the minimum but three is noticeably better in wind.
Arch3 ribs recommended
✓
Tie ribs together with connecting wire or rails
Run galvanized wire or a horizontal fence rail along the peak and along the sides to connect all three ribs. This stops the arch from racking front-to-back.
Arch
✓
Replace any temp zip ties with wire
UV kills zip ties in one season. Replace all structural zip ties with galvanized wire before calling the arch done. Zip ties can stay on mockup points you plan to revisit.
Replace zip ties
✓
Install front lower rails (left and right of door opening)
Complete the lower front frame on each side of the door opening. These carry the lower hardware cloth on the front face and define the door opening clearly.
Framing
✓
Apply hardware cloth to lower front zones
The two small panels between the outer posts and the door opening edges. Same staple-first-then-washer method as the sides. Fold any bottom edges under.
Mesh
✓
Build door frames — two identical leaves
Each door: two vertical stiles, top rail, middle rail, bottom rail. Width 22–24". Build mostly rectangular — ease the top corners only if it's easy. Square and plumb is the goal.
Doors
✓
Apply mesh to door panels
Hardware cloth on the lower half (below middle rail). Upper half: leave open to wire or use welded wire if you want more protection. Staple and washer same as everywhere else.
Mesh
✓
Hang doors with exterior hinges
Two heavy-duty exterior hinges per door minimum. Screw to the center post and the outer posts on each side. Check swing before final tightening. Leave a little gap — wood moves.
DoorsLeave swing gap
✓
Install latches — hook-and-eye or gate latch
Hook-and-eye on each door. If one leaf tends to blow open, add a cane bolt at the bottom to pin it to the bed frame. Test that both doors latch reliably from outside.
Doors
✓
Full wire edge audit — clip and fold all sharp ends
Walk the whole structure. Every cut cattle panel end and hardware cloth edge gets clipped flush or bent back. Do not skip this. You will hit these with your arm eventually.
Safety — do this
✓
Add optional bird netting over arch
If birds are a problem, drape lightweight bird netting over the cattle panel arch. Clip or tie at the edges. This is optional — if the enclosed structure is tight enough, birds usually won't get in.
Optional
✓
Replace any remaining temporary zip ties
Final check — anything you used zip ties on for mockup that didn't get replaced yet. Galvanized wire on all structural points before the structure is considered done.
Replace zip ties
✓
Test door operation — both leaves, both latches
Open and close both doors fully. Check that latches engage cleanly. Look for any racking or binding. Adjust hinge screws if needed. Doors should swing free and latch without forcing.
QC
✓
Plant and set up seedling protection
Basil, lettuce, and herbs in the front rows. Tomatoes and beans at the back where they'll climb the cattle panel. White mesh baskets or similar covers over small seedlings near the front.
Done
