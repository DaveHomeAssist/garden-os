## 2. Steam Long Description

**Inherit the Bed. Earn the Garden.**

Your mother kept a raised bed in the backyard of her South Philly rowhome. 8 feet by 4 feet. Thirty-two square feet of soil, sun, and whatever she was working through. She's gone now. The bed is yours.

Garden OS is a narrative strategy game played across 12 chapters and 3 real growing seasons. You will plan, plant, and defend a single raised bed — not a sprawling farm, not a fantasy meadow. One bed. The stakes are small. The decisions are not.

**PLAN EVERY SQUARE FOOT**
Each chapter opens with a planting phase. Choose what goes where. Companion planting matters — some crops protect each other, some compete for the same nutrients, some will sabotage your entire layout if you put them next to the wrong neighbor. Trellis placement blocks light. Root depth limits what follows. Every square is a commitment.

**THREE VOICES IN YOUR EAR**
- **Garden GURL** — The scoring judge. Dry, exacting, unimpressed by sentiment. She grades your bed on yield, biodiversity, and aesthetic composition. She does not care about your feelings.
- **Onion Man** — The emotional reactor. A Phillies fan who takes every wilted leaf personally. He will mourn your tomatoes. He will celebrate your peppers. He has opinions about mulch.
- **Vegeman** — The chaos tutorial agent. His advice is structurally correct and socially unhinged. He will teach you the game's systems while suggesting things no reasonable person would attempt.

**DETERMINISTIC SCORING, REAL CONSEQUENCES**
Nothing is random. Every outcome traces back to a decision you made. When a crop fails, you will know exactly why. When a chapter scores poorly, Garden GURL will tell you exactly where. The game respects your planning and punishes your guesses.

**SEASONAL EVENTS AND SOUTH PHILLY WEATHER**
Spring frost. August heatwaves. Block party weekend. The neighbor's cat. The calendar is not decorative — it is a constraint system. Each season introduces mechanical pressure that forces you to adapt or lose ground.

**12 CHAPTERS. 3 YEARS. ONE BED.**
The campaign is structured, authored, and finite. There is no endless sandbox. There is a story about a garden your mother kept, and whether you can keep it too.

## 5. Trailer Beat Outline — 60 Seconds

**0:00–0:10 — THE HOOK (described above)**
Empty bed. Seed packet. Garden GURL's opening line. Establish tone: this is specific, authored, not pastoral fantasy.

**0:10–0:20 — THE GRID**
Cut to the game UI — the 8x4 grid from above. A hand drags a tomato seedling into a square. Then a basil plant next to it. A subtle green line connects them — companion planting. Then a pepper placed nearby and a warning indicator appears. Onion Man's voice: *"You're gonna put the pepper THERE? Next to the — okay. Okay. This is fine."* Quick cuts showing 3-4 different planting configurations snapping into place.

**0:20–0:30 — THE SYSTEMS**
Trellis placement casting a shadow across two squares. A root-depth cross-section view showing crops competing underground. A calendar advancing — frost date approaching, marked in red. Vegeman's voice, fast and manic: *"Technically if you stagger the beets in a chevron pattern you gain half a pH point but you will lose your mind."* Show the scoring overlay — Garden GURL's grading matrix appearing over the bed, crisp and unforgiving.

**0:30–0:42 — THE SEASONS**
Montage across three years. Spring: bare soil, careful first plantings. Summer: the bed overflowing, green and dense. Fall: harvest, scoring, some squares empty. Winter: the bed under a thin layer of snow, the backyard quiet. Then spring again — different plants, higher difficulty. Intercut with chapter title cards: "Chapter 4: July, Year One." "Chapter 9: April, Year Three." Onion Man, quieter now: *"She grew the same tomatoes every year. Did you know that?"*

**0:42–0:52 — THE COMPANIONS**
Brief character moments. Garden GURL delivering a score with zero warmth. Onion Man reacting to a failed crop like a blown save. Vegeman drawing a diagram that makes no sense and perfect sense simultaneously. These are not tutorial NPCs. These are people with opinions about your dirt.

**0:52–0:60 — THE CLOSE**
Back to the overhead bed shot, now fully planted, mid-season. Hold for two beats. Then:

**TEXT ON SCREEN:**
GARDEN OS
*One bed. Twelve chapters. Thirty-two square feet.*

**Garden GURL (final line):** *"Let's see what you remember."*

## 6. Screenshot Strategy — Six Screenshots

**Screenshot 1 — "The Grid"**
The core planting UI. 8x4 bed from above, mid-plan. Some squares filled, some empty. Companion planting indicators visible. Sidebar showing available crops. This is the money shot — it communicates "this is a strategy game" immediately.

**Screenshot 2 — "The Score"**
End-of-chapter scoring screen. Garden GURL's portrait in the corner. Yield, biodiversity, and aesthetic scores broken out. One category marked excellent, one marked poor. This communicates deterministic feedback and that performance is graded.

**Screenshot 3 — "The Backyard"**
Wider view establishing the South Philly setting. The rowhome, the back fence, a glimpse of the neighbor's yard. The raised bed centered. Maybe a lawn chair. Concrete and brick. Not pastoral — urban, specific, real.

**Screenshot 4 — "The Conversation"**
A dialogue moment with Onion Man. His portrait, a line of dialogue that reveals character (something about the Phillies or about Mom). The bed visible in the background. This communicates that narrative is a real layer, not flavor text.

**Screenshot 5 — "The Problem"**
A mid-season crisis. The calendar showing an incoming heatwave. A trellis casting shadow in an unplanned direction. A crop stress indicator. This communicates that the game has pressure and requires adaptation.

**Screenshot 6 — "The Quiet Moment"**
The bed in winter. Snow on the soil. The backyard still. No UI overlay — or minimal. This communicates tone, emotional register, and the passage of time. This screenshot does the work of saying "this game has something to say."

## 7. Capsule Art Direction

**Main Capsule (Header):**
Overhead view of the 8x4 bed — stylized but grounded. Not pixel art, not photorealism. Something illustrative with texture, like a well-made seed catalog cover or a WPA poster sensibility. The bed should be mid-season, lush but contained. The South Philly backyard framing the edges — brick, concrete, a chain-link fence. Title treatment should be clean, typographic, no flourishes. The word GARDEN large, the word OS smaller and monospaced, as if it's a system designation.

**Small Capsule (Browse):**
Just the bed from above. Green on brown. The 8x4 grid faintly visible. Title overlaid. It must read at thumbnail scale — the geometric clarity of the raised bed shape is the visual hook.

**Color palette:** Earth tones grounded by concrete gray. Green as an accent, not a wash. Avoid the teal/pink/yellow cozy-indie palette entirely. This should feel closer to a Criterion Collection cover than a mobile game icon.

**Typography:** One clean sans-serif or slab-serif. Nothing handwritten, nothing whimsical. The game's personality comes from the writing, not the font.

## 8. What Press Will Latch Onto

The story press will tell about this game:

**"A strategy game about grief disguised as a gardening game."** This is the pitch that writes itself. Every preview will mention the mother. Every review will spend a paragraph on how the 8x4 constraint mirrors something emotional. The raised bed as inherited obligation, as memorial, as the bounded space of what one person can tend.

Secondary angles:
- **The South Philly specificity.** Games journalism is hungry for settings that aren't medieval Europe or space. A rowhome backyard in South Philly is a hook. Onion Man being a Phillies fan is a hook. The block party as a seasonal event is a hook. Specificity is coverage.
- **The anti-farming-sim positioning.** "It looks like Stardew but it's actually Into the Breach" is a sentence someone will write and it will drive wishlists.
- **The companion characters as design innovation.** Three NPCs who serve different mechanical roles (scoring, emotional feedback, tutorial) while being genuinely funny and distinct. Vegeman alone could generate social media traction.
- **Deterministic scoring in a genre that usually hides consequences.** The "nothing is random" angle appeals to the strategy-core audience that typically ignores anything with plants on the cover.

## 9. What Players Will Misunderstand (and How to Correct It)

**Misunderstanding 1: "This is a cozy farming sim."**
The art direction and subject matter will make people assume this is a relaxation game. Every piece of marketing must counterprogram this. Use words like "strategy," "deterministic," "scoring," and "consequences" early and often. The trailer should show failure states. The Steam tags must lead with Strategy, not Farming or Relaxation. Do not use the word "cozy" anywhere. Do not use the word "wholesome."

**Misunderstanding 2: "This is a sandbox / endless game."**
Players will assume they can garden forever. The 12-chapter structure must be communicated clearly — in the description, in the trailer, in the screenshot captions. "A 12-chapter campaign" is a selling point for the right audience and a necessary expectation-setter for everyone else. Frame the finite structure as authored and intentional, not as a limitation.

**Misunderstanding 3: "The characters are just tutorial pop-ups."**
Players who see Vegeman explaining a mechanic will assume he's a tooltip with a face. The marketing must show the companions having opinions, reacting to outcomes, and existing as characters beyond their mechanical roles. Dialogue screenshots should feature lines that reveal personality, not instructions.

**Misunderstanding 4: "It's small / there's not enough game here."**
An 8x4 bed sounds tiny. The marketing must reframe constraint as depth. "32 square feet" should feel like "32 decisions per chapter." Comparison language is useful here — chess is played on 64 squares, Go on 361 intersections. Small boards, deep games.
