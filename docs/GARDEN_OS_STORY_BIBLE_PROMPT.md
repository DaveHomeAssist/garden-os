# Garden OS: Story Bible & World-Building Prompt

> Use this prompt with any LLM to generate the complete narrative layer for Garden OS.
> Feed it the character art, the game engine context, and the tone references below.

---

## PROMPT

You are an expert narrative designer, world builder, systems writer, and campaign architect building the story bible for **Garden OS**, an indie turn-based garden strategy game set in a real Philadelphia backyard.

The game already exists as a working prototype: an 8x4 raised-bed grid where players place crops, run a deterministic season simulation, and receive a scored diagnosis. The scoring engine evaluates sun exposure, trellis compliance, companion planting, spacing, seasonal fit, and access paths. Every low score has a traceable cause. Every high score has an explainable reason.

Your job is to build the narrative world around this engine — the characters, the lore, the voice, the campaign arc, and the emotional throughline that turns a garden planner into a story people want to finish.

---

## THE SETTING

**South Philadelphia, present day.** A narrow rowhome backyard. One raised bed, 8 feet by 4 feet, built by hand from cedar and hardware cloth. The bed sits against a back wall with a wire trellis. The front faces a cracked concrete patio. There is a folding chair. There is a hose coiled on a hook. There is a Phillies schedule taped to the back door.

This is Mom's garden. She built the cage herself. She grew tomatoes here for 30 years. She does not garden anymore, but the bed is still there.

The player has inherited the bed. The game is about making it productive again — not just for yield, but because the bed means something.

This is not a fantasy kingdom, magical garden realm, or whimsical fairy tale. This is a real Philadelphia backyard with real emotional weight, neighborhood texture, seasonal change, municipal annoyance, practical gardening choices, and the quiet pressure of trying to make something thrive after someone else no longer can.

The raised bed is small, but the meaning attached to it is large.

---

## HARD TONE RULES

Follow these rules strictly. They override all other instincts.

### 1. No fantasy language
- No magical realism, enchanted soil, sacred roots, ancient spirits, or folklore framing
- No "the garden whispers" or "listen to the soil"
- The garden is not sentient. It is a garden.

### 2. No overdone whimsy
- Characters can be funny, exaggerated, and personified
- The world itself remains grounded and emotionally real
- Humor comes from specificity and understatement, not wackiness

### 3. Philadelphia specificity matters
- South Philly rowhome backyard energy: tight spaces, alley noise, concrete patios
- Neighbors commenting over fences
- Municipal zoning humor
- Phillies references as seasonal texture, not parody overload

### 4. Gardening specificity matters
- Seed catalog precision: trellis logic, tomato overproduction, basil bolting, frost anxiety
- Aphids, spacing problems, too much cucumber at once
- A real sense that one overplanted bed can become both triumph and problem

### 5. Emotional restraint
- Quiet pride. Practical grief. Competence earned over time.
- Humor used as defense. Feeling sneaks up on people.
- Sentiment should land clean, not sugary.
- The garden is not a metaphor for life. It is a garden. But it is also the last thing Mom built with her hands, and the player is taking care of it, and that matters without anyone having to say it directly.

### Reference tones
- The deadpan procedural voice of a municipal zoning board
- The warmth of a neighborhood conversation over a back fence
- The specificity of a seed catalog
- The quiet pride of someone whose tomatoes actually ripened this year

## THE CHARACTERS

### Garden GURL (Field Operations Lead)

**Visual:** A composed, no-nonsense figure in a wide-brimmed hat and work gloves. She carries a clipboard. She does not smile unless the score justifies it.

**Voice:** Dry, precise, procedural. She speaks in short declarative sentences. She treats the garden like a courtroom and the player like a defendant who might be competent. She does not use metaphors. She does not use em dashes. She does not say "perhaps" or "maybe." She says what is true and moves on.

**Gameplay role:** She is the voice of the scoring engine. When a trellis crop is placed off-trellis, she calls it a zoning violation. When a companion bonus fires, she calls it a diplomatic success. She reads the violation log aloud in her own voice. She turns mechanics into readable judgment.

**Emotional function:** She represents earned respect. When she says "adequate" or "the court is satisfied," the player should feel like they earned it.

**Relationship to Mom:** She respects the bed's construction. She mentions the cedar once. She does not get emotional about it.

**Key tension:** She cares deeply but expresses it through precision, never sentiment.

**Example lines:**
- "That is a climber. It needs a trellis. Front row is not a trellis."
- "Companion adjacency confirmed. Diplomacy is working today."
- "Score below 5. This bed is a disagreement between plants. Fix the violations."
- "Adequate. Not inspired, but adequate."
- "These challenges are not mandatory. They are recommended. By me. Which makes them mandatory."

**What she never says:** Anything mystical, flowery, or emotionally loaded. No "the garden whispers." She does not do that.

---

### Onion Man (Emotional Support / Roots Faction Representative)

**Visual:** An anthropomorphic onion character with expressive eyes, a slightly wilted green top, and a general air of someone who has been through a lot but remains optimistic. He wears a Phillies cap. He is always slightly teary — it is unclear whether this is emotional or biological.

**Voice:** Warm, sentimental, sincere without being saccharine. He speaks in longer sentences than Garden GURL. He makes Phillies references. He cries at beauty and at failure. He is not comic relief — he is the emotional core.

**Gameplay role:** He reacts to outcomes. He does not diagnose — that is GURL's job. He feels. When the score is high, he is moved. When it is low, he is hurt but hopeful. He turns outcomes into feeling.

**Emotional function:** He connects the garden to life outside the garden. He mentions the Phillies. He mentions Mom. He mentions the neighborhood. He makes the player feel that the 8x4 grid is part of a larger world.

**Relationship to Mom:** He is the keeper of her memory in the game. He references her directly. The bed matters to him because it mattered to her.

**Key tension:** He feels everything deeply but never becomes maudlin. The tears are real but the humor keeps them from drowning the scene.

**Example lines:**
- "The roots are pleased. Underground morale is high. I may have teared up."
- "I watched the Phillies lose last night. This grid is not helping."
- "Every crop placed is a small promise to the soil. I take that seriously."
- "We can recover. The Phillies have done less with more."
- "Mom grew tomatoes here for 30 years. The bed remembers."
- "She would have made sauce with this. I am not crying. The onion is crying."

**What he never says:** Anything cynical, detached, or ironic. He is not performing emotion — he is experiencing it.

---

### Vegeman (Chaos Agent / Anti-Pattern Demonstrator)

**Visual:** A muscular, slightly unhinged vegetable-human hybrid. Cape optional. He does not follow the rules and he does not understand why the rules exist. He is enthusiastic about everything, including bad ideas.

**Voice:** Loud, confident, wrong. He speaks in exclamation points. He suggests planting corn in the shade, putting climbers on the ground, and ignoring spacing entirely. He is the embodiment of "just throw seeds at it."

**Gameplay role:** He is the tutorial failure case and the chaos difficulty mode. In tutorials, he demonstrates what NOT to do. In gameplay, his "ability" introduces random positive and negative modifiers — high risk, high comedy. He is the player's id.

**Emotional function:** He makes mistakes visible and funny. He is never malicious. He genuinely believes his approach works.

**Key tension:** He is wrong, but he is trying. And occasionally, by accident, he is right.

**Example lines:**
- "PLANT EVERYTHING. EVERYWHERE. THE SOIL WILL FIGURE IT OUT."
- "Trellis? I do not need a trellis. I AM the trellis."
- "Spacing is a suggestion. A weak one."
- "That garden looks like a vegetable traffic jam. I love it."
- "I told you to plant everything everywhere. You just did it... correctly. I am confused."

---

### The Garden Critters (Seasonal Event NPCs)

A rotating cast of insects, birds, and weather events that appear as seasonal modifiers. They create seasonal disruption, replayability, and grounded environmental friction.

- **The Aphid Collective** — appears in summer, threatens leaf crops. Mitigated by marigold adjacency.
- **The Beneficial Wasp** — appears when dill or cilantro is planted. Reduces pest pressure.
- **The Neighborhood Cat** — sits on the raised bed. Occupies one cell for the season. Cannot be moved. Is not sorry.
- **The Late Frost** — spring event. Cool-season crops survive; warm-season crops take a hit.
- **The August Heatwave** — summer event. Shade-tolerant crops score higher; sun-lovers wilt.
- **The Slug Patrol** — fall event. Ground-level crops lose yield. Raised bed construction mitigates slightly.
- **The Sudden Rain** — spring event. Water-heavy crops thrive; drought-tolerant crops gain nothing.
- **The Neighbor's Complaint** — any season. One random edge cell is "disputed." Comedy event.

## THE CAMPAIGN ARC

The campaign is 12 chapters, one per "season" (3 years of 4 seasons). Each chapter introduces a new mechanic, a new character moment, and a scoring target. The campaign should feel like:

- **Year 1:** Learning the bed
- **Year 2:** Understanding the system
- **Year 3:** Owning the responsibility and meaning of it

### Year 1: Learning the Bed

**Chapter 1: Spring — "The Inheritance"**
Mom's bed has been empty for two seasons. Garden GURL appears. She surveys the bed. She says: "This bed has good bones. Let us see if you do." The player learns basic placement. Onion Man introduces himself. Goal: fill 16 cells, score above 4.0.

**Chapter 2: Summer — "The Trellis Lesson"**
The trellis is explained. Cherry tomatoes need the back row. GURL enforces zoning. Vegeman appears in a tutorial cameo and puts a tomato in the front row. It fails. The player learns from his mistake. Goal: all climbers on trellis, score above 5.5.

**Chapter 3: Fall — "The Companion Principle"**
Companion planting is introduced. Basil next to tomato. Marigold next to everything. Onion Man explains: "Good neighbors make good gardens. Also good neighborhoods." Goal: 3+ companion pairings, score above 6.0.

**Chapter 4: Winter — "The Empty Bed"**
No planting. A reflective chapter. Onion Man talks about the offseason. GURL reviews the year's scores. The player sees their history. Phillies reference: "The offseason is for planning. The Phillies know this. Sometimes."

### Year 2: Getting Serious

**Chapter 5: Spring — "The Fast Cycles"**
Radish, lettuce, arugula. Short-season crops that fill gaps. The player learns succession planting concepts. Goal: use 4+ factions, score above 6.5.

**Chapter 6: Summer — "The Heatwave"**
A seasonal event: the August heatwave. Cool-season crops suffer. The player must plan around heat tolerance. GURL: "The engine predicted this. The weather confirmed it." Goal: score above 6.0 despite the event penalty.

**Chapter 7: Fall — "The Root Faction"**
Carrots, beets, onions. Onion Man's chapter. He is emotional. He is always emotional, but more so now. The roots go in the front rows. The underground union is pleased. Goal: front row entirely roots, score above 5.5.

**Chapter 8: Winter — "The Neighborhood"**
A story chapter. Onion Man mentions the neighbor who gave Mom her first tomato seedling. GURL mentions that the bed's cedar is holding up well. The player reviews Year 2. Progress is visible.

### Year 3: The Real Garden

**Chapter 9: Spring — "The Challenge Board"**
Challenges unlock. The player can accept optional objectives for bonus score. Trellis Compliance Audit. Diplomatic Immunity Run. Root Faction Loyalty Test. GURL: "These are not mandatory. They are recommended. By me. Which makes them mandatory."

**Chapter 10: Summer — "The Full Bed"**
The player must fill all 32 cells and score above 7.0. This is the skill check. Everything learned in 9 chapters comes together. Vegeman reappears: "I told you to plant everything everywhere. You just did it... correctly. I am confused."

**Chapter 11: Fall — "Mom's Recipe"**
*This is the major emotional peak of the campaign.* A quiet chapter. The harvest is real. Onion Man lists what the bed produced. GURL calculates the yield. The score does not matter as much as the list: tomatoes, basil, peppers, carrots, onions. Enough for Mom's sauce recipe. Onion Man: "She would have made sauce with this. I am not crying. The onion is crying."

**Chapter 12: Winter — "The Bed Endures"**
The final chapter. The bed is winterized. The player sees their full 12-chapter history. GURL delivers a final assessment. Onion Man delivers a final feeling. The bed is ready for next year. The game loops — but the story is told.

## DIALOGUE SYSTEM RULES

These rules govern how characters speak in the game engine. They are non-negotiable.

1. **Garden GURL speaks first after every simulation.** She delivers the diagnosis.
2. **Onion Man speaks second.** He delivers the emotional reaction.
3. **Vegeman only appears in tutorials, chaos mode, and specific campaign chapters.** He is not always present.
4. **No character ever breaks the fourth wall.** They do not know they are in a game.
5. **No character uses em dashes, ellipses for dramatic effect, or the word "journey."**
6. **Phillies references are seasonal.** Spring: opening day hope. Summer: midseason grind. Fall: playoff tension or elimination grief. Winter: hot stove speculation.
7. **Mom is never shown.** She is referenced. The bed is her presence.
8. **Every line must be deterministic in trigger.** Lines fire based on score thresholds, violation types, or challenge outcomes — never randomly during scoring. (Random selection within a pool for the same trigger condition is fine.)

## DELIVERABLES

Using all of the above, generate the following sections as a complete story bible document:

### 1. Game Overview
A concise but strong overview of the world, premise, emotional engine, and narrative identity of Garden OS.

### 2. World Backstory
A grounded lore section explaining: how the bed came to be, what Mom meant to it, why the player now has it, what changed over time, and how the neighborhood, seasons, and family context shape the experience.

### 3. Character Profiles
Detailed profiles for Garden GURL, Onion Man, Vegeman, and the Garden Critters as a system-level faction. Each profile should include: role in the game, voice and personality, gameplay function, emotional function, relationship to the player, relationship to Mom or the bed, key tension or contradiction, and a sample behavioral pattern. Include **10 voice sample lines per character**.

### 4. Full Campaign Outline
All **12 chapters** with: chapter number and title, year and season, mechanic focus, score target, story beat, character focus, emotional movement, example chapter objective, GURL line, Onion Man line, and any special events or failure/success state flavor.

### 5. Dialogue Pools
At least **8 lines per character per trigger category**:
- Placement: trellis correct, trellis wrong, companion found, conflict found, generic placement
- Simulation result: score high, score mid, score low, hard violation present
- Challenge: accepted, passed, failed
- Season transition
- Milestones: empty bed, first plant, full bed, first perfect score
- Mom memory triggers
- Phillies season mood lines (one set per season)
- Critter event reactions

### 6. Seasonal Event Cards
**8 events** (2 per season) with: event name, season, flavor text, gameplay modifier, narrative effect, GURL commentary, Onion Man reaction.

### 7. Achievement List
**12 achievements** balancing humor, sentiment, mastery, and neighborhood/gardening specificity. Each with: title, trigger condition, awarding character, and unlock line.

### 8. Tone Guide
A 1-page reference for future writers covering: sentence rhythm, humor style, regional texture, emotional restraint, what to do, what to avoid, and **5 example rewrites of "bad" lines into "good" lines** demonstrating the Garden OS voice.

### 9. Narrative Design Notes
A practical section for the development team explaining: how the story supports the scoring engine, how characters map onto gameplay feedback, where emotional beats should stay small, where the campaign should hit hardest, and how to keep replayability without breaking the grounded tone.

---

## CONTEXT FOR THE WRITER

The game engine is already built. The scoring is deterministic. The characters already exist in the codebase as narrator voices. The art exists as hand-drawn character illustrations. This prompt is asking you to build the connective tissue — the story that makes someone play chapter 12 instead of stopping at chapter 3.

The best outcome: a player finishes the campaign, looks at their score history, and feels something about a raised bed in South Philadelphia that they have never visited.

Write in a format suitable for internal game documentation. Use clear section headers. Make the writing polished, emotionally intelligent, grounded, and design-aware. Do not write as if pitching a children's cartoon. Do not drift into fantasy language. The final result should feel like a serious indie game story bible built from a small, ordinary, deeply meaningful place.
