# Garden OS: Game Design Analysis & Loop Architecture

> Produced from prototype analysis of garden-league-simulator-v2.html and garden-league-simulator.html.
> This document defines the game's identity, core loop, tension systems, reward cadence, and the specific changes needed to move from scoring sandbox to narrative strategy game.

---

## High Concept

Garden OS is a grounded narrative strategy game where you inherit your mom's 8 by 4 raised bed and learn to care for it, one season, one layout, and one emotionally loaded decision at a time.

## Elevator Pitch

Garden OS is a small-scale, high-meaning strategy game set in a real South Philly backyard, where the player inherits a raised bed their mom no longer tends and must figure out how to make it thrive across seasons. Instead of fantasy farming sprawl, the game focuses on tight spatial planning, companion planting, trellis logic, weather events, pests, and hard tradeoffs inside a single 8 by 4 plot. Its voice is carried by a cast of grounded, strange, memorable characters — Garden GURL, Onion Man, Vegeman, and the Garden Critters — but the emotional weight comes from something simple and human: taking over a space that used to belong to someone you love, and slowly becoming worthy of it.

## What Makes It Emotionally Unique

Its emotion is not "wholesome gardening." Its emotion is **inheritance through maintenance**.

You are not building a dream farm. You are stepping into a space someone else made, someone who mattered, and discovering that care is a skill before it is a feeling. The bed becomes a stand-in for family memory, aging, responsibility, guilt, pride, and earned competence. The game does not sentimentalize the garden. It asks the player to prove love through attention, learning, restraint, and follow-through.

That is much rarer, and much stronger, than cozy nostalgia.

## What Makes It Mechanically Unique

Its best mechanical hook is **high-consequence micro-scale strategy**.

Most gardening games go wide. Garden OS goes deep. One raised bed. Limited cells. Real spacing. Trellis direction matters. Shade matters. Seasonal timing matters. Neighbors matter. Events matter.

The uniqueness comes from this combo:
- Tiny board, serious consequences
- Real horticultural logic translated into elegant strategy
- Narrative payoff generated by systemic success or failure
- Seasonal campaigns instead of endless sandbox drift

## Current Prototype State

**Current loop:** pick crop → choose season → place crops in 32 cells → optionally take one random challenge → run one instant season simulation → read average score, violations, buffs, weakest cell, narrator commentary, and score history → clear or auto-fill → try again.

The prototype labels itself "Place → Simulate → Improve." The state model is: planning / simulating / results.

Under the hood, each planted cell is judged on sun, support, shade, access, season, and adjacency, with adjacency shaped by companion links, conflicts, water mismatch, and crowding. That is good game material.

### Why It Risks Feeling Flat

1. **One button collapses the whole season.** No in-season tension, no pivot, no rescue, no "oh no" moment.
2. **The reward is mostly diagnosis, not transformation.** Excellent feedback, but weak payoff by itself.
3. **The challenge layer is thin.** Three simple layout constraints that validate after the fact.
4. **Time is not yet a real system.** `advanceTime` is a no-op. The game talks like a season sim but behaves like a static planner with scoring.

## Proposed Core Loop

**Plan → Commit → Weather the Season → Spend One Intervention → Harvest → Carry Forward**

### Start of Season
Choose the season goal, review inherited constraints, draft the bed.

### Commit Phase
Lock the layout. Once locked, most placements cannot be freely edited.

### Season Phase (3 Beats)
Early season, mid season, late season. At each beat, one event or condition resolves: heat wave, aphids, crowding, bolt risk, family interruption, rain week, neighborhood cat sitting on a square, etc.

### Intervention Phase
At each beat, the player gets one limited action:
- Prune
- Re-stake
- Swap one cell
- Sacrifice one plant
- Add mulch
- Companion patch
- Accept loss and protect yield elsewhere

### Harvest Phase
Convert the final bed into tangible outputs: yield, sauce ingredients, mom memory beats, confidence, seed knowledge, and challenge completion.

### Carry Forward
A few things persist to the next season: unlocked crop notes, bed condition, saved seeds, family recipe progress, and relationship to the space.

**Avoid:** Grind, craft, romance, decorate, fight.

## Tension Sources

| Source | How It Works |
|---|---|
| Space tension | Only 32 cells. Every placement costs opportunity. |
| Timing tension | Cool season vs summer vs succession. |
| Exposure tension | Back rows want support crops, front rows favor access, light gradients reward some positions. |
| Adjacency tension | Good combos help, bad neighbors punish, water mismatch creates friction, identical crowding creates soft inefficiency. |
| Intervention scarcity | You cannot fix everything. One or two seasonal saves, not infinite undo. |
| Emotional tension | You are not optimizing abstract squares. You are trying to become worthy of Mom's bed. |

## Reward Cadence

### Short-Term (within a season)
- "Row harmony" bonuses when a lane comes together
- Immediate projected yield shifts when a great adjacency lands
- Character callouts for smart decisions
- Event counterplay wins (saving basil during a hot week)
- Visible pantry progress (+2 sauce tomatoes)
- "Bed confidence" meter going up when a weak zone is fixed

### Mid-Term (across 1-2 seasons)
- Unlocking better crop notes and family annotations
- Gaining trust from Garden GURL's commentary
- Recipe milestones
- Bed improvements (trellis tune-ups, mulch slots)
- New challenge contracts
- Legacy photos or memories tied to specific crops

### Long-Term (across 3 years)
- Restoring the bed's identity
- Completing Mom-linked dishes and rituals
- Mastering each season type
- Earning "clean season" runs
- Unlocking harder inheritance scenarios
- Changing how the characters talk to you
- Building a final Year 3 capstone harvest that feels earned

## Replayability Systems

1. **Rotating seasonal briefs:** "Mom hated wasted trellis space." "This season needs onion yield." "Keep one access lane clean."
2. **Event seed system:** Each season draws from a small but legible deck of weather, pest, and family modifiers.
3. **Crop rotation memory:** Using the same pattern repeatedly creates diminishing returns or soil fatigue lite.
4. **Challenge ladders:** The current three challenge types are a seed — build them into a curated ladder with medals.
5. **Daily or weekly featured bed seeds:** Same bed, same brief, global comparison. Streamable without breaking tone.
6. **Post-season scoring archetypes:** "Trellis Lawyer," "Companion Whisperer," "Overcrowding Menace," "Sauce Maximizer."

---

## Fail States

### Frustrating (avoid)
- One bad hidden rule tanks the whole season
- Random critter event destroys the best row with no answer
- Full season restart after one mistake
- Too much punishment for experimenting
- Scoring that says you failed but does not tell you what tradeoff caused it

### Interesting (build toward)
- You save the season but miss the challenge goal
- One row underperforms, so you pivot yield elsewhere
- A good plan gets stressed by weather, and you must choose who to save
- You get a lower grade but unlock a memory or recipe ingredient
- You knowingly break one rule to protect a more valuable outcome

**Interesting failure says:** that hurt, but I learned something and I chose it.

## Three Scoping Tiers

### Minimal Scope
Loop: Draft bed → lock season → resolve 2 seasonal events → pick 1 intervention each event → harvest → score and reflect.

Adds to current prototype: layout lock, 2 event beats per season, 3 intervention types, harvest resource output, campaign map of 12 chapters.

### Ideal Indie Scope
Loop: Draft from a seasonal brief → lock bed → early/mid/late season events → scarce interventions → harvest into pantry/memory/mastery → next season unlocks new pressures.

Adds: 3-year campaign, crop rotation memory, scenario ladder, chapter-specific objectives, sauce/pantry/family progress, character commentary that evolves with player style.

### Breakout Scope
Loop: Seasonal planning plus live authored scenarios plus ranked seeded runs plus deep legacy carry-over.

Adds: daily challenge seeds, global scoreboards by brief, "bed stories" system that remixes events and family context, mastery codex, async community runs, high-quality post-run recap cards for sharing.

## 10 Implementation Priorities

1. **Replace freeform editing with commitment windows.** Let the player revise during planning, then lock. After lock, only limited interventions can change the bed. This instantly creates stakes.

2. **Break one season into 3 beats.** Early, mid, late. The single-click resolution is the biggest reason it reads like a planner.

3. **Add intervention scarcity.** One action token per beat. Now the game has triage.

4. **Make harvest tangible.** End on yield types, pantry count, sauce ingredients, memory fragments, chapter progress — not score alone.

5. **Turn weak cells into mini dramas.** Surface one "problem child" per beat and give the player a real choice about it.

6. **Add competing objectives.** A season should ask for more than "maximize average." Hit 6.8, harvest 5 sauce crops, keep trellis legal, preserve Mom's basil corner.

7. **Keep randomness low but legible.** Use forecastable event classes: likely heat, moderate pest pressure, storm chance, family interruption day.

8. **Build scenario-authored chapters first.** Sandbox later. The campaign teaches the bed as a dramatic object.

9. **Give each crop a role identity.** Anchors, climbers, fillers, protectors, risk crops, score stabilizers.

10. **Make the post-run screen a story of tradeoffs.** Best row, weakest relationship, overcommitted space, smartest sacrifice, what Mom would have noticed, what to watch next season.

## The Single Biggest Change

**Do not simulate the whole season in one click. Simulate it in three painful, readable beats.**

That is the transformation from planner to game.

---

## Design Pillar

**Make a tiny space feel emotionally meaningful and strategically dense.**

Everything serves that: UI, writing, tutorialization, event design, progression, art direction, pacing.

If the bed feels small but deep, you win. If it feels small and shallow, the whole concept collapses.

---

## Brand Protection Rules

1. Do not expand too quickly into multiple beds, property management, NPC relationships, crafting, animals, or house decorating. The discipline of the concept is the reason it has teeth.

2. Visually avoid "mobile cozy game" cues: overly rounded bubbly UI, generic pastel abundance, decorative clutter, fake rustic branding. Aim for tactile materials, readable systems, seasonal atmosphere, a little grit, strong visual hierarchy.

3. Failure should be poignant, not punishing. Bad layouts, pests, or poor timing should sting, but they should create story and learning, not just reset the player.

4. Optimize for quotable press language: "a strategy game about inheritance," "an 8x4 plot with the emotional weight of a whole family story," "gardening as spatial reasoning and care work."
