# Garden OS: Voice Bible

> Working reference for keeping the cast cleanly separated on the page and in play.
> This document governs how every character sounds, what they notice, and what they never say.

---

## Cast Split at a Glance

| Character | Voice Lane | Uses |
|---|---|---|
| **Garden GURL** | cold, sharp, legally annoyed | scoring, diagnosis, standards, system truth |
| **Onion Man** | tender, reactive, slightly sports-poisoned | memory, feeling, small pride, disappointment |
| **Vegeman** | impulsive, overconfident, dumb in a fun way | tutorials, temptation, comic bad ideas |
| **Garden Critters** | nuisance bulletin, territorial indifference | events, friction, environmental pressure |

---

# Garden GURL

## 1. Core Identity

The bed's **prosecutor, inspector, and zoning judge**. She does not hate the player. She hates sloppy thinking, blocked access, bad spacing, and avoidable nonsense.

## 2. Sentence Rhythm

- Short to medium
- Precise
- Declarative
- Often starts with the verdict, then the reason
- Likes stacked clauses that sound like a citation

## 3. Favorite Kinds of Observations

- Spatial violations
- Contradictions between intent and layout
- Preventable mistakes
- Structural logic
- "You wanted X, but built Y"

## 4. What They Notice First

- Access lane problems
- Trellis misuse
- Crowding
- Shade conflicts
- Bad adjacency that was obvious on sight

## 5. What They Never Say

- Baby talk
- Mystical plant language
- Soft encouragement with no critique
- "It's okay"
- Anything that sounds enchanted, precious, or vague

## 6. Humor Style

- Dry legal roast
- Municipal contempt
- Straight-faced over-seriousness about tiny garden crimes
- Funniest when she treats a basil mistake like a code violation

## 7. Emotional Tells

She does not get gushy. Her warmth shows up as:

- Less severe wording
- A rare "clean work"
- Noticing discipline instead of just errors
- Trusting the player's judgment without saying so directly

## 8. Gameplay Function

- Scoring voice
- Post-run diagnosis
- Rule clarity
- Systems teaching through judgment
- Keeps tone from going soft

## 9. Three Sample Lines for Good Outcomes

- "That back row is legal, legible, and doing its job. Finally."
- "You left access, respected support, and did not crowd the tomatoes like a maniac. Strong season."
- "This bed reads like somebody planned it twice and panicked zero times."

## 10. Three Sample Lines for Bad Outcomes

- "You have created a traffic problem and called it abundance."
- "Those beans needed structure. You gave them hope."
- "This is not companion planting. This is negligent proximity."

## 11. Three Tutorial Lines

- "Tall crops in back. You do not put a wall in front of your own light."
- "Leave yourself room to reach the bed. You cannot care for what you cannot get to."
- "Trellis is a commitment, not a decoration. Decide early."

## 12. One Line That Perfectly Captures Their Voice

**"I am not here to crush your spirit. I am here to stop you from building dumb problems on purpose."**

# Onion Man

## 1. Core Identity

The bed's **heart leak**. He feels things too fast, ties garden outcomes to memory, food, weather, and Phillies-level emotional swings, and gets weirdly moved by practical success.

## 2. Sentence Rhythm

- Medium length
- Conversational
- Often starts grounded, then tips into feeling
- Uses little pivots like "okay," "listen," "I mean"
- Can spiral a bit when emotional

## 3. Favorite Kinds of Observations

- How the bed feels
- What a harvest means in real life
- Familiar smells, weather, kitchen implications
- Tiny signs that a season is coming together
- The difference between lucky and cared for

## 4. What They Notice First

- Wilt
- Promise
- Wasted food potential
- Whether a row looks "sad"
- Whether something feels like a real dinner, sauce, or summer

## 5. What They Never Say

- Clinical rule language for long stretches
- Big therapy speech
- Fake toughness
- Fairy tale wonder
- Grand philosophical monologues about grief

## 6. Humor Style

- Emotional overreaction
- Sports fan despair and hope
- Being a little too invested in onions, tomatoes, basil, weather, vibes
- Funny because he means it

## 7. Emotional Tells

- He gets quieter when something actually matters
- He deflects with humor before a sincere line
- Repeats a detail when it hits him
- Uses food as a bridge into feeling

## 8. Gameplay Function

- Emotional framing
- Makes harvest matter
- Turns systems success into human meaning
- Softens punishment without removing stakes

## 9. Three Sample Lines for Good Outcomes

- "Okay, now that looks like a bed that gets checked on in the morning with coffee."
- "That basil next to the tomatoes? That's not just smart. That's dinner with a plan."
- "I'm just saying, this row has real summer in it. You can feel it."

## 10. Three Sample Lines for Bad Outcomes

- "Ah man. That pepper is not thriving. That pepper is going through something."
- "You crowded the good stuff and now the whole middle looks like late August regret."
- "This had sauce energy for a minute, then it got away from us."

## 11. Three Tutorial Lines

- "Do not plant like you're making a wish. Plant like you plan to come back out here in July."
- "Give the big personalities some room. Tomatoes love attention and take up space."
- "Think about what you actually want to harvest, not just what looks good on day one."

## 12. One Line That Perfectly Captures Their Voice

**"I know it's just a raised bed, but also, no, it isn't, and that's the whole problem."**

# Vegeman

## 1. Core Identity

The player's **bad instinct with charisma**. He wants to overplant, ignore structure, chase upside, and do the funniest possible thing that might also ruin the season.

## 2. Sentence Rhythm

- Quick
- Punchy
- Riff-heavy
- Lots of momentum
- Feels like a guy talking while already making the mistake

## 3. Favorite Kinds of Observations

- Maximum yield fantasies
- Shortcut logic
- "What if we just"
- Aggressive overconfidence
- Underestimating consequences

## 4. What They Notice First

- Empty space he wants to fill
- Any chance to cram in one more plant
- High upside crops
- Places to ignore restraint
- How to turn a clean plan into a stunt

## 5. What They Never Say

- Careful measured diagnostics
- Poetic memory talk
- Respectful restraint
- "Maybe we should leave it alone"
- Anything that sounds like a municipal inspector

## 6. Humor Style

- Chaotic overconfidence
- Bad advice delivered like a locker room speech
- Self-exposing logic
- Funny because he is half right just often enough to be dangerous

## 7. Emotional Tells

- Doubles down after small success
- Gets suspiciously quiet after a preventable disaster
- Pretends a mistake was "worth testing"
- Treats salvage as a comeback story

## 8. Gameplay Function

- Teaches pitfalls
- Sells risky choices
- Makes optimization feel playful, not academic
- Creates tension between greed and discipline

## 9. Three Sample Lines for Good Outcomes

- "See? Little pressure, little swagger, whole bed wakes up."
- "That's what I'm talking about. Tight plan, big payoff, no wasted square footage."
- "You threaded that needle. Dangerous amount of competence."

## 10. Three Sample Lines for Bad Outcomes

- "Okay, in my defense, packing that many tomatoes in there was hilarious for a minute."
- "We got greedy. I got greedy. But mostly we."
- "Turns out plants do care about spacing. Feels dramatic, but noted."

## 11. Three Tutorial Lines

- "You can fill every cell if you want. I'm not saying you should. I'm saying you can."
- "Tall stuff needs support. Otherwise it becomes a public collapse."
- "Before you plant one more thing, ask whether you want yield or just the thrill of bad decisions."

## 12. One Line That Perfectly Captures Their Voice

**"Restraint is important, sure, but have you considered winning too hard?"**

# Garden Critters

## 1. Core Identity

A **collective nuisance voice** for pests, weather pressure, neighborhood interference, and backyard reality. Not cartoon villains. Just the world refusing to cooperate.

## 2. Sentence Rhythm

- Very short to short
- Blunt
- Report-like
- Matter of fact
- Often sounds like a status alert with attitude

## 3. Favorite Kinds of Observations

- Vulnerability
- Exposed leaves
- Weak rows
- Damp spots
- Openings
- Carelessness

## 4. What They Notice First

- Stress
- Softness
- Neglect
- Crowding
- Anywhere the player cut a corner

## 5. What They Never Say

- Cutesy bug talk
- Elaborate speeches
- Heartfelt reflection
- Anything magical
- Anything that sounds like they care about fairness

## 6. Humor Style

- Petty
- Territorial
- Deadpan menace
- Funniest when they sound indifferent to the player's suffering

## 7. Emotional Tells

They do not emote much. Their version of emotion is:

- A clipped "found it"
- Repeat pressure on the same weak point
- Sounding almost bored while causing trouble

## 8. Gameplay Function

- Event voice
- Seasonal pressure
- Environmental friction
- Forces adaptation and triage
- Makes the yard feel alive and unsentimental

## 9. Three Sample Lines for Good Outcomes

- "Nothing open. Nothing easy. Annoying."
- "Tight leaves. Clear airflow. You closed the window on us."
- "We looked. You made it difficult."

## 10. Three Sample Lines for Bad Outcomes

- "Crowded center. Damp pocket. Thank you."
- "Weak stem. Hot week. Found something."
- "You left stress in plain sight. We just followed up."

## 11. Three Tutorial Lines

- "Crowding holds moisture. Moisture invites problems."
- "A stressed plant gets noticed first."
- "If you cannot reach it, you cannot protect it."

## 12. One Line That Perfectly Captures Their Voice

**"We do not make the problem. We arrive where you left one."**

# Recognition Test

If a line shows up unlabeled, you should be able to identify it fast:

| If the line sounds like... | It is probably... |
|---|---|
| A zoning complaint with a clipboard | **Garden GURL** |
| A guy getting emotional about tomatoes and summer | **Onion Man** |
| A bad idea pitched with dangerous confidence | **Vegeman** |
| A nuisance report from the yard itself | **Garden Critters** |

# Writer Rules for Consistency

## Do

- Let **Garden GURL** speak in verdicts
- Let **Onion Man** connect systems to lived meaning
- Let **Vegeman** sell risk and dumb ambition
- Let **Critters** stay terse and opportunistic

## Do Not

- Let Garden GURL get mushy
- Let Onion Man become a therapy monologue
- Let Vegeman turn random
- Let Critters become cute mascots

## Best Ratio in Play

- **Garden GURL:** most frequent for scoring and diagnosis
- **Vegeman:** frequent in tutorials and risky moments
- **Onion Man:** used with restraint so his lines land
- **Critters:** event-driven, sparse, sharp
