FRONT DOORS

Door operation basics

  • Open one door for quick harvesting. Open both when you want full reach across the bed.
  • Close and latch both doors after every visit. A half-closed door is basically an invitation.
  • If one leaf drags, check mulch buildup at the threshold first, then hinge screws.
  • Do not force a sticky latch. Realign it now so it does not get worse later.

How to think about the cage

  • Back row: the climbing wall. That is where the tall summer workhorses belong.
  • Front zone: the safe, easy harvest area. Greens and herbs live here because critters love them too.
  • Doors: your access lane. Use that convenience when you decide what gets planted closest to the front.
02

Planting Zones and What Goes Where

Use the structure the way it was designed. Climbers in the back. Low crops in the protected front. Frequent harvest crops closest to the doors.

Planting map Companions
>
🪴 Trellis zone
Back row climbing crops

Cherry tomatoes, pole beans, peas, and cucumbers belong here. These crops use the rear trellis wall for support and keep the rest of the bed open.

🛡 Protected zone
Front protected crops

Basil, lettuce, bok choy, cilantro, radish, and arugula thrive here. They stay easy to reach and benefit most from critter protection.

✋ Daily use logic
Keep easy harvests up front

Anything you snip often or re-sow often should stay near the doors so the cage feels convenient, not annoying.

Companion planting notes

  • Beans + lettuce: a reliable pairing. Beans grow up, lettuce stays low, and the bed stays balanced.
  • Marigolds: tuck these near the front edge for visual structure and everyday pest pressure help.
  • Nasturtium: use as a trap crop and a pretty front-row filler. It also earns its keep by attracting attention away from more valuable crops.

03

Seasonal Playbook

What the cage wants from you across the year. Use this as the rhythm of the bed instead of reinventing the plan every month.

Spring to fall Timeline
>

Seasonal timeline

A simple year-round rhythm that matches how the cage actually gets used.

Spring

Start cool crops and set the bed up for succession.

Early summer

Shift into warm-season climbers and train the back row.

Late summer

Manage heat, watch for bolting, and seed the fall reset.

Fall

Bring the cool crops back and stretch the season.

Spring

  • Plant first: peas, lettuce, arugula, bok choy, cilantro, and radish.
  • Succession pace: lettuce every 2 weeks, radish every 3 weeks, cilantro every 3 weeks.
  • Frost note: if a late cold snap threatens, throw row cover inside the cage instead of over the whole structure.

Early summer

  • Transition: pull tired cool crops as they fade and make room for basil and warm-season growth.
  • Back row: plant or train cherry tomatoes, cucumbers, and pole beans onto the trellis.
  • Trellis habit: check and guide climbers twice a week until they are clearly attached and moving up.

Late summer

  • Heat management: keep mulch in place and water deeply, not constantly.
  • Watch for bolting: cilantro, arugula, and lettuce will tell you when they are done. Believe them and re-seed later.
  • Fall setup: start new rounds of bok choy, lettuce, cilantro, and arugula as temperatures ease.

Fall

  • Cool crops return: this is the second great season for greens.
  • Season extension: row covers inside the cage help a lot and are easy to manage.
  • Front zone strength: the protected area becomes the star again once the summer climbers wind down.
04

Maintenance Checklists

Small checks keep the cage reliable. Most problems are easy if you catch them early and annoying if you do not.

Monthly Seasonal
>

Monthly walk-through

  • Wire inspection: look for pulled staples, gaps, rust, or spots where the hardware cloth is starting to bow.
  • Door check: make sure hinges are tight, latches still line up, and the swing path is clear.
  • Trellis ties: replace degraded zip ties with wire or stronger ties and check the attachment points that carry the load.
  • Mesh audit: clip or fold any new sharp edges that show up after trimming or repair work.

Soil and bed care

  • Top-dress compost: once in spring, once midseason if the bed is producing hard, and once before fall greens go in.
  • Mulch: keep a light mulch layer in place to steady moisture and reduce splashback onto the greens.
  • Harvest hygiene: pull yellowing leaves and spent plants before they become clutter and slug housing.
05

Critter Defense Protocol

Know what the cage stops well, what it does not stop, and how to tell when something found a way in anyway.

Defense Escalation
>

What the 24 inch hardware cloth band stops

  • Rabbits
  • Ground squirrels and similar low-entry pests
  • Most casual chewing at ground level

What it does not stop on its own

  • Deer if the top is open and they decide the bed is worth it
  • Birds
  • Insects
  • Anything entering through a door that was left unlatched

Signs of a breach

  • Chewed leaves close to the soil line
  • Fresh disturbed soil near the edge
  • Droppings inside the protected zone
  • A sudden clean haircut on tender greens

Escalation steps

  • Add bird netting if fruit or seedlings are getting pecked.
  • Add row covers inside the cage if insects or cold snaps become the issue.
  • Add extra mesh or repair banding if a repeated ground-level breach shows up in the same area.
06

Harvest and Succession Guide

Keep the cage productive by harvesting the right way and replanting before empty space turns into wasted time.

Harvest Repeat sowing
>

Cut-and-come-again crops

  • Lettuce and arugula: cut outer leaves first and leave the center growing.
  • Bok choy: harvest young whole plants or thin outer leaves if you want a longer run.
  • Cilantro: harvest lightly and often before it rushes to bolt in heat.

When to pull and replant

  • Pull spent plants once production clearly drops or bolting starts.
  • Do not leave tired spring crops occupying prime front-zone space into hot weather.
  • As soon as a square opens, decide whether it gets another fast sowing or waits for the next seasonal shift.

07

End of Season Shutdown

Close the season cleanly so spring starts from a strong position instead of a mess you have to negotiate with.

Shutdown Winter prep
>

Season closeout

  • Pull annuals: remove dead or exhausted warm-season plants from the trellis and front rows.
  • Cut back the trellis: clear dead vines and any ties that no longer belong there.
  • Clean wire and mesh: remove plant debris so moisture does not sit on the hardware all winter.

Hardware and structure check

  • Oil hinges: a quick pass keeps the doors civilized.
  • Check post connections: tighten what loosened during the season.
  • Optional winter move: add season extension covers inside the cage if you want greens to keep going.
