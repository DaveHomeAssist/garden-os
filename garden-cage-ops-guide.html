Short Verdict

Build the structure like a small farm tunnel with a critter-proof skirt — not like a decorative garden feature. Five to six posts, 24" hardware cloth band, three cattle panel ribs, two simple front doors, no fancy carpentry. This is the version most likely to work, look decent, stay within reason, and not become a cursed side quest.

Assumption: Two 4×4 raised beds already exist side by side, giving a combined footprint of roughly 96" wide × 48" deep. Structure sits flush with the outer edges of the beds.

Critical Decisions — Decide Before Cutting

Post count
Go with 6 posts
Back center post costs one 2x4 and prevents wobble, racking, and panel droop. Skip it and you'll add it later anyway.
Rib count
3 ribs, not 2
Two ribs flex in wind. Three ribs make a rigid tunnel. The third panel costs ~$25 — worth it.
Lower band height
24 inches, no less
Rabbits jump 18". Squirrels climb from ground level. 24" with ½" hardware cloth stops both. Below 24" and you'll be patching gaps all season.
Door style
Two doors, not one
Single wide door racks and sags. Two 22–24" doors on a center post stay square and latch cleanly. Each door is light enough to build flat on the ground.
Post attachment
Screw to bed sides
Burial is overkill for a raised bed cage. Screw posts directly to the outer bed frame. Easier to build, easier to remove if needed.
Mesh fastening
Staples + fender washers
Staples alone pull out within a season. Staple to position, then screw fender washers every 8–12". Two-step process — no shortcuts.
Common Failure Points
01
Staples only, no washers. Hardware cloth pulls free within one season. Animals find the gap. Fender washers every 8–12" are non-negotiable.
02
Zip ties left as permanent fasteners. UV degrades them in months. Every structural tie must be galvanized wire. Zip ties are for mockup positioning only.
03
Skipping the back mesh panel. "They won't get in from the back." They will. Hardware cloth the lower 24" on all four sides, no exceptions.
04
Single wide door. A 44"+ single door racks, sags, and stops latching within weeks. Two narrower doors on a center post stay square.
05
Not checking plumb before screwing off posts. A leaning post cascades into a crooked frame, misaligned doors, and panels that don't seat. Check both axes, brace, then fasten.
06
Cutting before measuring the actual beds. DIY beds are rarely exact. Measure, mark your numbers on the sketch, then cut. Assumptions cost lumber.
07
Unclipped wire edges. Cut hardware cloth and cattle panel ends are razor-sharp. Every edge gets clipped flush or bent back before anyone works near the structure.
